SiteTrace watches every server, switchgear cabinet, and steel beam on its way to your data center build — from the factory door, across the ocean, through your gate, to its final spot on site. It notices trouble while there is still time to do something about it.

Section 01 — Why this exists

Late costs money.
Lost causes late.

Data center construction runs on a brutal clock. Every late week costs real money — idle crews, pushed-back commissioning, delayed go-live. And almost every late week starts the same way: material that isn’t where the schedule needs it to be. Three blind spots cause most of it:

“Where’s my shipment?”

Your parts cross oceans in other people’s ships and ride in other people’s trucks. Each carrier sees its own little piece. Nobody — until now — could see all of it on one screen, or which delay actually threatens the schedule.

Blind spot № 1 — In transit

“It arrived broken. Nobody knew.”

A server can be shaken to death inside a sealed crate and look perfect from the outside. Traditionally you discover that at installation — weeks later — when nobody can prove who dropped it or when.

Blind spot № 2 — Damage

“It’s here… somewhere.”

A construction yard is a small city of identical crates. Things get put down in the wrong place and vanish on your own property. Crews burn hours hunting for parts the company already owns — or re-order them.

Blind spot № 3 — On site

SiteTrace closes all three gaps with one system — and mostly without people typing anything.

Section 02 — The big idea

Everything is a sighting.

Every device in the system — the gate reader, the tracker riding on a pallet, a worker’s phone — produces the exact same small record: what was seen, where, when, how it was seen, and how sure we are. Every sighting flows into one ledger, in order, forever. Nothing is ever edited or erased.

Fig. 02-A Three kinds of eyes, one memory. No feature ever asks which device saw an item — only how sure the sighting was.

Why one ledger matters: because the value is in the connections. A hard jolt recorded at sea automatically forces an inspection at your gate. A ship running late automatically rolls up as schedule risk against the build plan. A pallet that stops being seen automatically becomes a task for a human to go look.

And because the ledger is append-only, it doubles as evidence. When you file a damage claim against a carrier, you attach the actual jolt — measured in G-force, timestamped, located on the map — not an argument. When a sighting is uncertain, SiteTrace doesn’t guess: it opens a small task asking a person to confirm with a 10-second phone scan.

Section 03 — Follow one shipment

The story of SHP‑0042

Sixteen servers leave a factory in Shenzhen, headed for server row A1–A4 at Granite Ridge. Here is exactly what SiteTrace does at every step.

Before we begin — about this data SHP-0042 is a demonstration scenario. The vendors, ships, and sensors are simulated; no real freight was harmed. But everything you see is the real SiteTrace application doing real work — every screenshot below is the live system processing that mock shipment exactly as it would a real one.
01

Shenzhen — vendor’s loading dock

Tagged before it ships

The vendor sticks a smart label on every server — a radio tag with a printed QR code, the item’s fingerprint. A reusable condition tracker rides with the crated load: a palm-sized box that reports its position and how gently (or roughly) it’s being handled, every few minutes, over the cellular network. The vendor also sends an electronic packing list, so the site knows exactly what’s coming before it arrives.

02

Pacific Ocean — aboard Evergreen Ocean

On the water, on the map

Every shipment in flight is a dot on one world map — ocean, air, and road together. Each dot carries a live arrival estimate, and when a vessel slips or customs holds a container, the dot turns amber and the delay flows straight into the build schedule (more on that in the next section).

03

Port of Los Angeles — crane transfer

The drop

During the transfer to a drayage truck, the pallet takes a 9.2 G jolt — nearly twice what a server is built to survive (the alarm threshold for servers is 5 G). The tracker felt it and phoned home within minutes.

Here’s the part that usually takes lawyers: whose hands was it in? SiteTrace already knows. The tracker’s GPS trail shows the pallet had left the ship and crossed into the drayage company’s custody zone. The damage event is automatically pinned to PacWest Drayage — no one had to log a handoff.

04

Still at sea level — seconds later

One jolt, sixteen flags

SiteTrace knows what was packed in that load, because packing was recorded as an event too. So the single jolt automatically flags all 16 servers travelling with it. Each one is now “damage-hold”: it must pass a physical inspection before the site will accept it. The project team gets the alert the same minute — days before the truck reaches the site, not weeks after installation.

05

Granite Ridge — main gate

Received at driving speed

The truck rolls through the gate without stopping. The RFID portal over the entrance reads every smart label aboard in one pass and checks the load against the vendor’s packing list. No clipboard, no unloading-and-counting.

One server doesn’t read — radio waves and dense metal don’t always cooperate, and SiteTrace expects that. Since the server was packed in a crate that did read, it’s marked “almost certainly here” and a 10-second verification task is queued for a human. Discrepancies open tasks; they never block the truck.

06

Laydown Yard A

It files itself on the yard map

A forklift sets the pallet down in Laydown Yard A. Nobody types anything: the tracker keeps pinging from the pallet, the ping lands inside the yard zone drawn on the site map, and the placement is recorded automatically. From now on, anyone can search a serial number and get a zone — plus the item’s complete location history on the property.

07

Receiving apron — inspection bay

The check-up, with receipts

The sixteen flagged servers can’t be accepted until someone looks at them. Each inspection is a guided pass/fail with notes and photos. Fifteen pass. One doesn’t — and a claim opens against PacWest Drayage automatically, with the 9.2 G reading, the timestamp, and the GPS location already attached. The argument about “who broke it” is over before it starts.

The happy ending A yard worker gets the verification task from step 05, walks to the crate, scans the missed server’s QR code with a phone — and the exception clears itself. Fifteen healthy servers head to the rack; the sixteenth is already being replaced on the carrier’s dime. And the build schedule knew about all of it from the moment of the jolt — that’s the next section.

Section 04 — The build schedule

Every crate knows
its deadline.

The schedule screen is where logistics meets construction. Every build activity — “Erect Mezzanine Steel,” “Install Server Row A1–A4,” “Energize Switchgear Lineup A” — carries a need-by date, and every tracked part is linked to the activity waiting on it.

  • — Need-by dateThe day the material must be on site for the crew. Everything is measured against it.
  • — FloatDays of slack between when a part will arrive and when it’s needed. “Install Server Row A1–A4” is showing −2 days — the troubled servers from Section 03 put it behind. Caught this early, it’s a recoverable problem.
  • — Critical pathStarred activities have zero room: if they slip, the whole project slips. SiteTrace watches their materials hardest.
  • — What-ifBefore committing to a change, ask: “if this shipment slips ten days, which activities move?” The answer comes back in seconds, not in a planning meeting.

Section 05 — The control room

One screen to start the day

Everything in the story above rolls up here. A coordinator’s morning begins with a handful of numbers and a “needs attention” list — not with phone calls.

  • — Shipments in transitFive on the move; two waving a flag (customs hold, weather delay).
  • — Open exceptionsThe system’s to-do list for humans: 28 small judgment calls, sorted by severity.
  • — Damage & inspectionsThree open damage events; the queue of check-ups they triggered.
  • — Needs attentionThe short list a human should read first — shocks, silent trackers, missing items — newest and worst on top.

Return № 1 — Claims that stick

A damage claim backed by a timestamped, GPS-located G-force reading gets paid; a hunch doesn’t. One recovered claim on a single damaged server rack can exceed a year of tracker subscriptions.

Return № 2 — Hours & re-orders that vanish

No more crews walking the yard hunting crates, and no more re-ordering parts the company already owns. Time-to-locate drops from hours to one search box.

Return № 3 — Slips caught early

The expensive delay is the one you learn about last. Every day of warning is a day to re-sequence crews, expedite a replacement, or re-plan. One critical activity kept on time can repay the entire system.

Section 07 — Fleet health

Watching the watchers

A tracking system is only as honest as its devices. The Devices tab is the registry of the whole capture fleet — every tracker and the gate portal — with the three things that matter: battery, last seen, and who it’s assigned to.

  • — Lifecycle poolTrackers cycle: available → assigned → in transit → returned to the charger, then out again on the next pallet. The registry shows where every unit sits in that loop.
  • — Battery watchEvery ping reports charge. Devices below 15% surface automatically, before they die in the field.
  • — Silence is an alarmA tracker that misses its check-in window — like TRK-0007 above — raises an alert. A quiet device might mean a dead battery, a stripped pallet, or a crate that left the property.
  • — The portal tooPORTAL-GATE-1 reports its own health the same way. If the gate reader ever goes dark, you know before the next truck arrives — not after.

Section 08 — The people

Software does the typing.
People do the judging.

Notice what’s missing from this manual: data entry. Nobody fills in spreadsheets, nobody logs handoffs, nobody counts boxes off a truck. The devices do the bookkeeping.

What people do is work the exception queue: the short list of judgment calls the system won’t make alone. “I’m 76% sure this busway arrived — can someone confirm?” “This tracker went quiet — can someone check the pallet?” Each card is a two-minute job with a resolve button, designed for a phone in the yard, and often cleared by simply scanning the item in question.

The same phone-first screens cover inspections (guided pass/fail with photos) and the scan page’s four verbs: verify, receive, put away, locate. If the day ever goes sideways, everything the devices do, a person with a phone can do manually — the system treats both identically.
